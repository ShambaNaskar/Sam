A brief overview on Work, Energy and Power

  • What is work?

    Work, in the context of physics, is defined as the product of the force applied to an object and the distance over which the force is applied in the direction of the force. Mathematically, work

    W=F⋅d⋅cosθ

    Where: W is the work done, F is the force applied, d is the displacement of the object, θ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.

    Work is a scalar quantity measured in joules (J) in the International System of Units (SI). It represents the energy transferred to or from an object by the application of force over a distance.

  • Units of work:

    The unit of work is the joule (J) in the International System of Units (SI). One joule is equal to the work done when a force of one newton is applied over a distance of one meter in the direction of the force. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

  • 1joule (J) = 1newton (N) × 1meter (m)

  • Other units of work include the erg (1 erg = 10−7joules) and the foot-pound (1 foot-pound = 1.35582 joules), although the joule is the most commonly used unit in physics and engineering contexts.

  • Dimensions of Work:

    The dimension of work can be derived using the formula for work, which is given by: Work = Force × Distance

    The dimension of force is mass × acceleration (M L T-2), and the dimension of distance is length (L). Therefore, the dimension of work is:

    Work = Force × Distance = (M L T-2) L = M L2 T-2

    This means that the dimension of work is mass × length squared × time squared (M L2 T-2).

  • What is Power?

    The definition of power is the rate at which work is done or the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. It is expressed as the amount of work done per unit time or the amount of energy transferred per unit time. Mathematically, power (P) is defined as: P = (W / T).

  • SI Units:
    • The SI unit of power is the watt (W), which is equivalent to one joule per second (1 W = 1 J/s).
  • Other Units:
    • In the CGS (centimeter-gram-second) system, the unit of power is the erg per second (erg/s).
    • In the FPS (foot-pound-second) system, the unit of power is the foot-pound per second (ft-lb/s) or horsepower (hp).
  • Dimensions of Power:

    The dimension of power in the SI system is [M L2 T-3], representing mass, length, and time raised to the respective powers.

  • Concept of kinetic energy:

    Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion. It is defined as the work required to accelerate an object of a given mass from rest to its current velocity. The kinetic energy of an object depends on its mass and the square of its velocity and is expressed mathematically as:

  • Kinetic Energy(KE) = (1 / 2).m.v2

  • Kinetic energy is a scalar quantity and is measured in joules (J) in the SI system. It is an essential concept in physics, particularly in understanding the energy associated with moving objects and their ability to do work.

  • Concept of potential energy:

    • Potential energy is a form of energy that an object possesses due to its position or configuration in a force field. It is energy that is stored in the object and can be released or converted into other forms of energy. The concept of potential energy is based on the idea that certain forces, such as gravitational force or elastic force, can do work on objects and store energy in them.

  • There are different types of potential energy depending on the type of force involved:

  • Gravitational Potential Energy:

    This type of potential energy is associated with the gravitational force between objects. The gravitational potential energy of an object near the surface of the Earth is given by the formula:

  • Pgravitational = mgh

  • where m is the mass of the object, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and h is the height of the object above a reference point.

  • Elastic Potential Energy:

    This type of potential energy is associated with elastic forces, such as those in a spring or a stretched rubber band. The elastic potential energy of an object that can be stretched or compressed is given by:

  • Pelastic = (1 / 2)kx2

  • where k is the spring constant (a measure of the stiffness of the spring) and x is the displacement of the object from its equilibrium position.

  • Chemical Potential Energy:

    This type of potential energy is associated with chemical bonds in molecules. When chemical reactions occur, the potential energy stored in the bonds of reactants can be released or converted into other forms of energy.

  • Electric Potential Energy:

    This type of potential energy is associated with the electric force between charged objects. The electric potential energy of charged particles in an electric field is given by:

  • Pelectric = k (q1.q2) / r

  • where k is Coulomb's constant, q1 and q2 are the charges of the objects, and r is the distance between them.

  • These examples illustrate how potential energy can be understood in different contexts, depending on the forces and interactions involved.

  • Dissipation of Energy:

    Dissipation of energy refers to the process where energy is lost or converted into forms that are not useful for the intended purpose. In various systems and machines, energy dissipation occurs due to factors such as friction, heat transfer, air resistance, and other non-conservative forces. This dissipated energy often results in an increase in entropy, contributing to the overall entropy production in the system.

  • Efficiency of a machine:

    is a measure of how effectively it converts input energy into useful output energy. It is typically expressed as a ratio of output energy to input energy, multiplied by 100 to represent it as a percentage. The efficiency η of a machine is given by the formula:

  • η = (Input energy / Useful output energy) × 100%

  • If a machine has an efficiency of 90%, it means that the machine can convert 90% of the input energy into useful output energy. The remaining 10% of the input energy is lost or dissipated as waste energy, often in the form of heat, sound, or other non-useful forms.

    For instance, let's consider a car engine with an efficiency of 90%. If the engine consumes 100 units of fuel energy (input energy), it can convert 90 units of that energy into mechanical work to move the car forward (output energy). The remaining 10 units of energy are lost as heat, friction, and other inefficiencies within the engine and its components.

  • What is Conservative Force?

    A conservative force is a force field in which the work done by the force on an object moving between two points is independent of the path taken and depends only on the initial and final positions of the object.

  • The mass-energy equivalence principle, famously expressed by Einstein's equation E = mc2, states that mass and energy are interchangeable and equivalent. This means that mass can be converted into energy and vice versa, with the conversion factor being the speed of light squared (c2).

  • Elastic and Inelastic Collision:

    In elastic collisions, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved, meaning that the total kinetic energy of the system remains constant before and after the collision.

    In inelastic collisions, momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not. Some of the kinetic energy is transformed into other forms of energy, such as thermal energy or deformation energy, during the collision.

    • Understand the Concepts: Develop a clear understanding of work, energy, and power concepts, including their definitions, units, and calculations.
    • Practice Problem-Solving: Regularly practice solving numerical problems involving work done, energy transformations, and power calculations to strengthen your problem-solving skills.
    • Master Energy Conservation: Focus on the principle of conservation of energy and how it applies to various systems, including potential and kinetic energy conversions.
    • Study Different Forms of Energy: Learn about different forms of energy such as mechanical, thermal, electrical, and their interconversion processes.
    • Learn Power Calculations: Understand the concept of power and its relation to work and energy, including the formulas for power calculations in different contexts.
    • Use Diagrams and Graphs: Utilize diagrams and energy transfer graphs to visualize energy transformations and understand the work-energy theorem.
    • Review Work-Energy Applications: Study practical applications of work and energy principles in various scenarios like simple machines, mechanical systems, and energy conservation devices.
    • Apply Physics Laws: Apply relevant physics laws and principles, such as the work-energy theorem and the laws of thermodynamics, to solve complex problems effectively.
    • Practice Time Management: During exams, allocate sufficient time to solve energy-related questions, focusing on accurate calculations and logical reasoning.
    • Use Mock Tests: Take advantage of mock tests and practice papers available online to assess your understanding and enhance your preparation for energy-related topics.
    • Seek Guidance: Seek guidance from teachers, peers, or online resources to clarify doubts, understand difficult concepts, and improve your overall grasp of work, energy, and power.
    • Stay Updated: Stay updated with any changes or updates in the NEET syllabus related to work, energy, and power topics to ensure comprehensive preparation for the exam.
