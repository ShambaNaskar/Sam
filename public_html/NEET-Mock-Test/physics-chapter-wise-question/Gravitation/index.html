A brief overview on Gravitation

  • Newtons Law of Gravity:

    The Newton's Law of Gravity states that every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

    F = G (m1.m2) / r2

    Where: F is the gravitational force between two objects, G is the universal gravitational constant, m1 and m2 are the masses of the two objects, r is the distance between their centers.

    This law is fundamental to understanding the gravitational interactions between celestial bodies and plays a crucial role in various aspects of physics and astronomy.

  • universal gravitational constant G:

    Definition: The gravitational constant (G) is a fundamental constant in physics that quantifies the strength of the gravitational force between two objects. It appears in Newton's law of universal gravitation and in Albert Einstein's general theory of relativity

    The value of the universal gravitational constant G is approximately 6.67430 × 10-11 m3 / kg s2.

    The dimensions of the universal gravitational constant G are [ M-1L3T-2 ].

  • Gravitation field and Gravitation Potential:

    The gravitational field is a vector field that describes the force experienced by a mass at any point in space due to the presence of another mass. It is represented by the gravitational field strength (g) and is directed towards the mass causing the field.

    Gravitational potential, on the other hand, is a scalar quantity that represents the work done in moving a unit mass from infinity to a point in the gravitational field. It is denoted by V or ϕ and is measured in joules per kilogram (J/kg) or volts (V).

    The relationship between gravitational field and gravitational potential is given by the equation:

    g = (dr / dV)

    where g is the gravitational field strength, V is the gravitational potential, and r is the distance from the center of mass causing the field. The negative sign indicates that the gravitational field points towards regions of lower gravitational potential.

  • The acceleration due to gravity (g) varies under different conditions:

    Height: As you move away from the Earth's surface, the gravitational acceleration decreases. This is because the gravitational force weakens with distance, following an inverse square law.


    Depth: At greater depths below the Earth's surface, the gravitational acceleration decreases slightly. This is due to the mass of the Earth above exerting a lesser gravitational pull compared to when you are closer to the surface.


    Spinning of Earth: The rotation of the Earth affects the effective gravitational acceleration slightly. Objects at the equator experience a centrifugal force due to the Earth's rotation, which reduces the net gravitational force experienced.


    Ellipsoidal shape of Earth: The Earth's shape is not a perfect sphere but slightly flattened at the poles and bulging at the equator. This non-spherical shape causes variations in gravitational acceleration across different latitudes.


    These variations are relatively small and often negligible for everyday purposes, but they are important considerations in precise scientific calculations and measurements.

  • Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion:

  • Kepler's laws of planetary motion are fundamental principles describing the motion of planets around the Sun. They were formulated by Johannes Kepler based on observational data collected by Tycho Brahe. Here are Kepler's three laws:

  • Law of Ellipses: The orbit of a planet around the Sun is an ellipse, with the Sun at one of the two foci of the ellipse.
  • Law of Equal Areas: A line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. This means that planets move faster when they are closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when they are farther away (aphelion).
  • Law of Harmonies (or Law of Periods): The square of the orbital period of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit. Mathematically, it can be expressed as T2 = a3 , where T is the orbital period and a is the semi-major axis of the orbit.

  • These laws provided a significant breakthrough in understanding the dynamics of planetary motion and laid the foundation for Isaac Newton's laws of motion and universal gravitation.

  • What is Escape Velocity?

  • Escape velocity is the minimum velocity an object needs to escape the gravitational pull of a celestial body, such as a planet or a moon, without being pulled back. It is the speed required for an object to break free from the gravitational influence and move into space indefinitely. The escape velocity depends on the mass and radius of the celestial body. Mathematically, the escape velocity vescape can be calculated using the formula:

    vescape = √(2GM / r)

    For Earth, the approximate escape velocity is about 11.2 kilometers per second (km/s) or approximately 33 times the speed of sound in air.

  • A geostationary or parking orbit

    A geostationary or parking orbit refers to a specific circular orbit around Earth at an altitude where the orbital period matches the rotational period of the Earth. This means that a satellite in a geostationary orbit appears to remain stationary relative to a fixed point on Earth's surface. Geostationary orbits are commonly used for communication satellites because they allow antennas on Earth to point directly at the satellite without needing to track its movement.

  • A geostationary satellite is a satellite that is placed in a geostationary orbit. These satellites are positioned at approximately 35,786 kilometers (22,236 miles) above Earth's equator. They complete one orbit around Earth in the same amount of time it takes Earth to complete one rotation on its axis, which is approximately 24 hours. This synchronization allows the satellite to remain fixed relative to a specific point on Earth, making them ideal for applications like telecommunications, weather monitoring, and broadcasting.

  • A polar satellite refers to a type of Earth observation satellite that orbits the Earth in a north-south direction, passing over both the North and South Poles during each orbit. Unlike geostationary satellites, which remain fixed relative to a point on Earth's surface, polar satellites follow a polar orbit that allows them to cover the entire Earth's surface over time.

  • What is Polar satellites?

    Polar satellites typically orbit at lower altitudes compared to geostationary satellites, which allows for higher resolution imaging and data collection. They are commonly used for various purposes such as weather monitoring, environmental monitoring, scientific research, and mapping. Due to their orbital characteristics, polar satellites can provide global coverage and gather valuable data about Earth's surface, atmosphere, and climate.
