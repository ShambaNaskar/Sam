A brief overview on Thermodynamics

  • Welcome to the fascinating realm of thermodynamics, where we delve into the intricate workings of heat, energy, and their transformations. In this chapter, we explore fundamental concepts such as thermal equilibrium, the definition of temperature, heat transfer mechanisms, and the laws governing energy interactions within systems. Join us as we unravel the mysteries of thermodynamics and discover the profound impact of these principles on our understanding of the physical world.

  • Mechanical equivalent of Heat:

    The concept of the mechanical equivalent of heat reveals the intimate connection between mechanical work and heat energy. It suggests that a specific amount of mechanical work can generate or be equivalent to a certain amount of heat energy. This principle is crucial in understanding the relationship between different forms of energy and has significant implications in various fields, from engineering to physics and beyond.

  • First Law of Thermodynamics:

    The first law of thermodynamics, often called the law of energy conservation, asserts that energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system; it can only change forms or be transferred from one part of the system to another. In essence, this law emphasizes the principle of energy conservation, stating that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant over time. It forms the foundation of thermodynamics and plays a crucial role in understanding various processes, including heat transfer, work, and internal energy changes within systems.

  • The Internal Energy of a Gas:

    The internal energy of a gas refers to the sum of the kinetic and potential energies of its individual molecules. It encompasses the energy associated with the molecular motion, rotation, and vibration within the gas. This internal energy contributes to the overall thermodynamic properties of the gas, including its temperature, pressure, and heat capacity.

  • Limitations of First law of thermodynamics:

    It does not specify the efficiency of energy conversion processes: The first law only accounts for the total energy transfer in a system but does not indicate how efficiently this energy is converted or utilized.

    It does not address the irreversibility of processes: The first law treats all processes as reversible, whereas in reality, many processes are irreversible, leading to energy losses and entropy generation.

    It does not consider the quality of energy: The first law treats all forms of energy (e.g., heat, work) as equivalent, without distinguishing between high-quality energy (useful for work) and low-quality energy (difficult to convert into useful work).

    It does not explain spontaneous processes: The first law does not provide insights into why certain processes occur spontaneously while others require external intervention, which is addressed by the second law of thermodynamics.

  • External Work Done by a Gas:

    The external work done by a gas refers to the mechanical work performed by the gas on its surroundings or by the surroundings on the gas during a process. This work can result from changes in volume (expansion or compression) of the gas against an external pressure.

  • The Specific Heat of a Gas:

    It refers to the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of the gas by one degree Celsius or one Kelvin. It is a measure of the gas's thermal capacity or its ability to absorb heat without a significant change in temperature.

    There are two specific heat capacities associated with gases:

  • 1. Specific Heat at Constant Volume (Cv):

    This is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of the gas at constant volume. Mathematically, it is given by:

    Cv = Q / (m.ΔT)

    Where, Q is the heat added, m is the mass of the gas, and ΔT is the change in temperature.

  • 2. Specific Heat at Constant Pressure (Cp):

    This is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of the gas at constant pressure. Mathematically, it is given by:

    Cp = Q / (m.ΔT)

    Where, Q is the heat added, m is the mass of the gas, and ΔT is the change in temperature.

  • The ratio of Cp to Cv for an ideal gas is denoted by the symbol γ. Mathematically, the ratio is given by:

    γ = Cp / Cv

    For monatomic ideal gases (like helium or neon), the value of γ is approximately 5/3, or 1.67. For diatomic ideal gases (like nitrogen or oxygen), γ is around 7/5, or 1.4. These values may vary slightly depending on the gas and the conditions, but they provide a general idea of the ratio of specific heats for different types of gases.

  • Isothermal Process:

    An isothermal process is a thermodynamic process during which the temperature of a system remains constant. This means that the internal energy of the system remains constant throughout the process. In an isothermal process, heat is added or removed from the system to maintain a constant temperature, usually in an environment with good thermal conductivity.

  • Adiabatic Process:

    On the other hand, an adiabatic process is a thermodynamic process during which no heat is exchanged between the system and its surroundings. This means that the change in internal energy of the system is solely due to work done on or by the system. Adiabatic processes are often rapid and occur in insulated systems where heat transfer is negligible.

  • Comparison: Adiabatic Process vs Isothermal Process
    Adiabatic Process Isothermal Process
    Definition A thermodynamic process in which no heat exchange occurs with the surroundings (Q = 0). A thermodynamic process that occurs at constant temperature (ΔT = 0).
    Heat Transfer No heat transfer occurs (Q = 0). Heat transfer may occur to maintain constant temperature (Q ≠ 0).
    Temperature Change Temperature change may occur (ΔT ≠ 0). Temperature remains constant throughout the process (ΔT = 0).
    Work Done Work may be done or performed on the system. No work is done if the volume remains constant (W = 0).
    Equation PVγ = constant, where γ is the ratio of specific heats (γ = CP/CV). PV = constant (Boyle's Law), or PV = nRT for an ideal gas (Charles's Law).
    Applications Used in processes like adiabatic compression or expansion in engines and turbines. Used in processes like refrigeration and heat pumps.
    Example Air compression in a diesel engine. Expansion of gas in a refrigerator.
  • Reversible Process:

    A reversible process is a thermodynamic process that can be reversed without leaving any effect on the surroundings or the system. In a reversible process, the system proceeds through a series of equilibrium states, and the system and surroundings can return to their original state after the process is reversed. Reversible processes are idealized concepts and are used in theoretical thermodynamics to establish upper limits and ideal conditions for various thermodynamic processes.

  • Irreversible Process:

    An irreversible process is a thermodynamic process that cannot be reversed to its original state without leaving a permanent change in the system or surroundings. Irreversible processes involve dissipative effects such as friction, heat transfer across finite temperature differences, and irreversibilities in flow processes. Real-world processes are often irreversible due to factors like friction, fluid flow resistance, and heat transfer through finite temperature gradients.

  • Comparison: Reversible Process vs Irreversible Process:
    Reversible Process Irreversible Process
    Definition A process that can be reversed without causing any changes to the system or its surroundings. A process that cannot be reversed to its original state without affecting the system or its surroundings.
    Direction Can proceed in either direction without causing any net change. Proceeds in only one direction, leading to an irreversible change.
    Entropy Change No net change in entropy (ΔS = 0). Results in an increase in entropy (ΔS > 0).
    Energy Transfer Energy transfer is efficient and can be reversed completely. Energy transfer is often inefficient and leads to energy dissipation (e.g., heat loss).
    Examples Quasistatic processes, ideal gas expansions or compressions with infinitesimal changes. Real-world processes like combustion, irreversible expansions, and irreversible chemical reactions.
    Efficiency Can achieve maximum possible efficiency under ideal conditions. Efficiency is typically lower due to energy losses and irreversible changes.
    Applications Used in theoretical thermodynamics and idealized models. Common in practical engineering systems and natural processes.

  • The second law of thermodynamics is a fundamental principle that deals with the direction of natural processes and the quality of energy transformations. It can be stated in various forms, but one of the most commonly used statements is:


    "In a closed system, natural processes tend to increase the overall entropy (disorder) of the system and its surroundings over time."

  • Clausius Statement:

    "It is impossible to construct a device that operates on a cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a cooler body to a hotter body."

  • Kelvin-Plank Statement:

    "It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the extraction of heat from a single thermal reservoir and the complete conversion of this heat into work."

  • What is Entropy?

    Entropy is a fundamental concept in thermodynamics that quantifies the amount of disorder or randomness in a system. It is a measure of the system's microscopic configurations or the number of ways in which its particles can be arranged while still maintaining the same macroscopic properties (such as temperature, pressure, and volume).


    In simple terms, entropy can be understood as a measure of the system's "disorderliness" or the amount of energy in a system that is not available to do work. When a system undergoes a spontaneous process, the entropy of the system and its surroundings tends to increase, leading to an overall increase in disorder.


    Entropy is denoted by the symbol S and is typically expressed in units of joules per Kelvin (J/K) in the International System of Units (SI). It plays a crucial role in various thermodynamic processes, such as heat transfer, energy conversion, and the directionality of natural processes governed by the second law of thermodynamics.

Some Important Tips:

  • Focus on Laws of Thermodynamics:

    The first and second laws of thermodynamics are fundamental. Understand their applications in various processes, such as heat engines, refrigerators, and entropy changes.

  • Master Heat Engines and Cycles:

    Study the working principles of heat engines, especially the Carnot cycle and its efficiency.

  • Understand Entropy and Reversibility:

    Gain a clear understanding of entropy and its role in determining the direction of natural processes. Also, learn about reversible and irreversible processes.

  • Learn about Ideal Gases and Processes:

    Focus on ideal gas behavior under different processes like isothermal and adiabatic processes.

  • Efficiency and Heat Transfer:

    Understand the concepts of efficiency, heat transfer mechanisms, and the principles behind heat pumps and refrigeration cycles.

    Practice solving numerical problems related to thermodynamics, especially those involving heat transfer, work done, and energy conversions. Our website's mock tests offer practice questions to test your understanding and problem-solving skills.
