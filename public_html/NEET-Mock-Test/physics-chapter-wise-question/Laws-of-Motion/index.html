A brief overview on "Newton's Laws of Motion"

  • What is force?

    Force is a physical quantity that describes the interaction between objects, causing a change in their motion or state of rest. It is a vector quantity, which means it has both magnitude and direction. Force is measured in units such as Newtons (N) in the International System of Units (SI).

  • What is momentum?

    Momentum is a fundamental concept in physics that describes the quantity of motion an object possesses. It is defined as the product of an object's mass and its velocity. Mathematically, momentum (p) can be expressed as: p = m × v
    Where: p is the momentum, m is the mass of the object, and v is the velocity of the object.

  • Statements of Newton's three laws of motion:
  • First Law (Law of Inertia):

    "An object at rest will remain at rest, and an object in motion will continue to move at a constant velocity along a straight line, unless acted upon by an external force."


    Explanation: This law highlights the concept of inertia, which is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. It implies that objects naturally maintain their current state of motion unless an external force is applied to alter that state.

  • Second Law (Law of Acceleration):

    " The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This relationship is expressed by the equation F=ma, where F is the net force, m is the mass of the object, and a is the acceleration produced."

  • Third Law (Action and Reaction):

    "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. When one object exerts a force on another object, the second object exerts an equal force in the opposite direction."


    Explanation: This law emphasizes the principle of action and reaction, stating that all forces occur in pairs. When one object applies a force on another, the second object simultaneously applies an equal but opposite force on the first object. These forces act on different objects and do not cancel each other out because they are exerted in opposite directions.

  • What is inertia of matter?

    The inertia of matter, often referred to simply as inertia, is the property of matter that resists changes in its state of motion or rest. It is a fundamental concept in physics and is described by two aspects: inertia of rest and inertia of motion.

  • Inertia of Rest: The inertia of rest refers to the tendency of an object to remain at rest or maintain its current state of rest unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Inertia of Motion: The inertia of motion (or simply inertia) refers to the tendency of an object in motion to continue moving at a constant velocity along a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.

  • Inertia of matter encompasses both inertia of rest and inertia of motion, reflecting the resistance of objects to changes in their states of motion or rest unless influenced by external forces according to Newton's first law of motion.

  • What is impulsive force?

    An impulsive force, also known as an impulse, refers to a force that acts on an object for a short duration of time, resulting in a change in the object's momentum. Impulsive forces are characterized by their brief application and can cause significant changes in an object's motion.

  • The concept of impulse is closely related to Newton's second law of motion ( F = m a ), which states that the force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of its momentum. Mathematically, impulse (J) is defined as the product of the force (F) and the time interval (Δt) over which the force acts:
    J=F⋅Δt

    Impulsive forces can lead to various effects on objects, such as changes in their velocities, directions of motion, or overall momentum. In physics, understanding impulsive forces is essential for analyzing situations involving collisions, impacts, or sudden changes in motion, where the duration of force application plays a crucial role in determining the resulting effects on the objects involved.

  • Reaction in Moving Lift: In a moving elevator, the occupants feel a force pushing them either downward (during upward motion) or upward (during downward motion). This sensation is caused by the reaction force exerted by the elevator floor on the occupants, which is necessary to maintain their equilibrium relative to the elevator's accelerating frame of reference.
  • Apparent Weight: The apparent weight of a person inside a moving elevator is the perceived weight they experience, which may differ from their actual weight when the elevator is at rest. During upward acceleration, the apparent weight increases as the reaction force from the elevator floor adds to the person's actual weight. Conversely, during downward acceleration, the apparent weight decreases as the reaction force partially counteracts the person's actual weight.
  • Law of conservation of linear momentum: The law of conservation of linear momentum is a fundamental principle in physics that states that the total linear momentum of a closed system remains constant if no external forces act on it. In other words, in the absence of external forces, the total momentum before an event (such as a collision or explosion) is equal to the total momentum after the event.
  • Motion of an object or a system of objects with variable mass: The motion of an object or a system of objects with variable mass involves scenarios where the mass of the system changes during motion. One classic example of this concept is the motion of a rocket or a jet plane.
    In the case of a rocket, as it burns fuel and expels it out of the engine, the mass of the rocket decreases over time. This change in mass affects the rocket's motion according to the principle of conservation of momentum.
  • Here's how the motion of a rocket illustrates the concept:

    • When a rocket or jet plane burns fuel and expels it, the mass of the system decreases. This change in mass affects the system's motion according to the law of conservation of momentum. As fuel is burned and ejected, the system experiences acceleration due to the thrust generated. The decreasing mass leads to an increase in acceleration, demonstrating the variable mass effect. This dynamic process of mass change influences the system's velocity to maintain momentum conservation throughout its motion.

  • What is friction?

    Friction can be defined as the force that opposes the relative motion or tendency of motion between two surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the surfaces and is caused by the irregularities or roughness present on the surfaces.

  • Static Friction: Static friction is the frictional force that prevents the relative motion between two surfaces when they are not moving relative to each other. It arises when an object is at rest on a surface and you apply a force to move it.
  • Sliding or Kinetic Friction: Sliding or kinetic friction is the frictional force that opposes the motion of two surfaces sliding or moving relative to each other. It occurs when an object is already in motion on a surface.
  • Limiting Friction: Limiting friction refers to the maximum frictional force that can be exerted between two surfaces before one of them starts sliding over the other. It depends on the nature of the surfaces in contact and the normal force pressing them together.
  • The law of friction: The law of friction, commonly expressed as F=μ.R, relates the frictional force (F) between two surfaces to the normal force (R) pressing them together and the coefficient of friction (μ) that represents the surface's roughness or smoothness. This law states that the frictional force is directly proportional to the normal force and is also influenced by the nature of the surfaces in contact, as indicated by the coefficient of friction.
