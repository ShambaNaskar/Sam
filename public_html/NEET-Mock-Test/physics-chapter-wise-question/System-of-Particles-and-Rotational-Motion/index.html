    • A brief overview on System of Particles and Rotational Motion

    • The chapter "System of Particles and Rotational Motion" delves into the study of objects in motion, both linearly and rotationally. It explores the dynamics of systems comprising multiple particles and the principles governing rotational motion. This chapter is fundamental in understanding the behavior of objects in various physical scenarios, from simple linear motion to complex rotational dynamics.

    • What is Rigid Body?

      A rigid body is a physical object whose shape and size remain constant, regardless of the forces applied to it. It does not deform or change internally when subjected to external forces. This concept is often used in physics to simplify the analysis of mechanical systems, especially in cases where deformation effects can be ignored.

    • What is Center of Mass?

      The center of mass (COM) of an object or system is the point where the entire mass of the object or system can be considered to be concentrated. It's a theoretical point that represents the average position of the mass distribution in the object.

    • What is Moment of Force?

      The moment of force, also known as torque, is a measure of the rotational effect of a force about an axis or pivot point. It's calculated as the product of the force applied and the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line of action of the force. Mathematically, torque (τ) is given by the formula:

      τ =Force × Perpendicular distance from axis of rotation

      The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter (Nm).

    • What is centre of gravity?

      The center of gravity is the point in a body or system of bodies where the entire weight of the body or system can be considered to act. It's the point at which the total gravitational force on the body is considered to be concentrated. For symmetrical objects with uniform density, the center of gravity is usually located at the geometric center of the object. However, for irregularly shaped objects or objects with non-uniform density, the center of gravity may be located at a different point.

    • Comparison: Centre of Mass vs Centre of Gravity
      Centre of Mass Centre of Gravity
      Definition The point at which the entire mass of an object can be considered to be concentrated. The point at which the entire weight of an object can be considered to be concentrated.
      Calculation Calculated based on the distribution of mass within an object. Calculated based on the distribution of weight due to gravity within an object.
      Location Always lies on the axis of symmetry if the object has one. May or may not coincide with the centre of mass depending on the gravitational field.
      Application Used in dynamics and kinematics calculations. Used in statics and equilibrium calculations.
      Effects of External Forces Not affected by external forces if the object is in free space. Can shift due to external forces acting on the object.
      Example The centre of mass of a uniform ring is at its geometric centre. The centre of gravity of a tilted object shifts towards the side with more weight.
    • Equilibrium of a Body under Gravity:

      Equilibrium refers to the state where a body experiences no net force or torque, resulting in no linear or angular acceleration. In the context of gravity, equilibrium can be achieved when the forces acting on a body due to gravity are balanced.

      ⬥ Stable Equilibrium: In stable equilibrium, a small displacement from the equilibrium position causes a restoring force or torque that brings the body back to its original position. For example, a ball sitting at the bottom of a concave bowl represents stable equilibrium.

      ⬥ Unstable Equilibrium: In unstable equilibrium, a small displacement from the equilibrium position causes a force or torque that further displaces the body away from its original position. An example is a ball sitting at the top of a convex surface.

      ⬥ Neutral Equilibrium: In neutral equilibrium, a small displacement from the equilibrium position does not cause any net force or torque. The body remains at rest in its new position without any tendency to return to the original position. An example is a ball placed on a flat surface.

    • What is Rotational Inertia?

      Rotational inertia, also known as moment of inertia, is a measure of an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion. It depends on both the mass of the object and its distribution around the axis of rotation. The formula for rotational inertia is:

      I = ∫ r2 . dm

      Where:

      I is the rotational inertia or moment of inertia.

      r is the distance of each particle of mass

      dm from the axis of rotation.

      dm is an infinitesimally small mass element of the object. In simpler terms, rotational inertia describes how difficult or easy it is to change the rotation of an object. Objects with larger rotational inertia require more torque to change their angular velocity, while objects with smaller rotational inertia can change their rotation more easily.

    • Law of conservation of Angular Momentum:

      The law of conservation of angular momentum states that the total angular momentum of a system remains constant if no external torque is applied to the system. In other words, if the net external torque acting on a system is zero, the angular momentum of the system is conserved.

      Mathematically, the law of conservation of angular momentum can be expressed as:

      Linitial = Lfinal

      This law is analogous to the conservation of linear momentum in translational motion, where the total linear momentum of a system remains constant if no external force is applied. The conservation of angular momentum is a fundamental principle in physics and is applicable in various situations, such as in rotational dynamics, celestial mechanics, and fluid dynamics.
